What is Accessibility?

Accessibility refers to the process of removing barriers to ensure that everyone has equal ability to access and use the same resources without the need for adaptation. Often, the needs of people with varying disabilities are overlooked in the design of common systems and infrastructure, which entirely renders them inaccessible. This is why the conversation of accessibility must be centralized in any form of design process. There is legislation in place to uphold accessibility, such as the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), a major civil rights law enacted to protect the rights of Americans with disabilities and hold agencies accountable for their inaccessible design practices. However, it is the responsibility of engineers, writers, and career professionals to guarantee that these legislation and accessibility practices are being upheld in their work.

Digital Accessibility

As more of our lives move to the digital world and screens, it’s essential to ensure that accessibility moves into these spaces as well. Digital accessibility refers to mandating that the standards of websites and online resources are made to create ease of use for all people, with or without disability. Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) have been instituted to comply with the ADA guidelines and create a singular standard for web content that meets the needs of all individuals. The 4 principles of web accessibility (POUR) are a part of WCAG to hold companies accountable for their digital accessibility, or lack thereof. Each principle refers to a specific aspect of accessible design and should be followed to ensure inclusivity:

o Perceivable refers to making sure those who have visual and hearing impairments can see and hear your website.

o Operable refers to all website features being navigable for all people.

o Understandable refers to clarity in the layout and language of your website.

o Robust refers to the wide range of assistive technologies and software your website should accommodate.

Adding a few features such as captions or alt-text, is not sufficient for inclusive design. Following the principles of POUR and ensuring websites comply with WCAG is essential for the future of digital accessibility.

Medical Model of Disability

The medical model of disability focuses on the personal impairment of a disability rather than the failures of an inaccessible environment. The model uses certain thresholds as “normal” and then individually measures those with disabilities up to these standards. This method is primarily used in the medical field to diagnose patients or for policymakers to create disability legislation. However, this model has been adopted far beyond these fields and has begun to take root in societal practices as well. As a result, the focus is shifted towards the individual with a disability having to adapt to an inaccessible world rather than highlighting the faults of society. This model further isolates those with disabilities and perpetuates a narrative that continues to ignore accessibility as a necessity within our world.

Social Model of Disability

The social model of disabilities aims to be the inverse of the medical model and focuses on the shortcomings of society when it comes to accessibility. The lack of ramps or elevators is the issue, not those who are unable to use the stairs. The blame shifts to the environments constructed in an inaccessible manner and forces people to confront accessibility as an essential process of innovation and design.
